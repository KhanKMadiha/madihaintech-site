What it does

Most professionals know what they want to say. The gap is how they say it: the clarity, the confidence, the delivery. Articulate is built around a simple habit: read aloud every day, in your own voice, on topics that matter to your career.

Every day the app generates a new passage using Claude, personalised to your job title, industry, career goals, and the speakers you most want to sound like. You record yourself reading it, and get AI feedback on how closely you followed the text, how fluent your delivery was, and whether your pace was right.

The goal is to make deliberate vocal practice as habitual as going to the gym.

How I built it

01

A coffee chat conversation

Where the idea came from

A senior engineering manager told me something that stuck: "You need to learn how to speak different languages. To customers, to engineers, to execs. They all need something different."

I wanted to build something to practise that, daily and deliberately. Reading aloud in your own voice, on topics that matter to your actual career, felt like the right habit to build.

02

React, Express and the Claude API

Started in Cursor, continued in Claude Code

I built the passage generation pipeline, recording flow, and AI scoring end to end. Each passage is personalised to the user's job title, industry, goals, and the speakers they admire — generated fresh every day by Claude.

Recording uses the Web Speech API with real-time transcription. The transcript is compared against the original passage to score accuracy, fluency, and pace — with specific, constructive feedback, not just a number.

The app also tracks streaks, lets users save favourite passages to a library, and supports push notifications as an optional daily reminder.

03

Dyslexia-friendly mode

A complete reading experience redesign, not just a font toggle

I added a full dyslexia-friendly reading mode as a named feature within the app. One paragraph at a time, a reading ruler that follows your position, live word highlighting as you speak, syllable break markers, and an OpenDyslexic font option.

The AI passages themselves are also adjusted in dyslexia mode — shorter sentences, simpler vocabulary, and avoiding easily-confused homophones — so the accessibility goes all the way through to the content.
